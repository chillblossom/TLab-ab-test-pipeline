# Stakeholder Questions — A/B Test Analysis



## Context Setting
Establish what was already done before this A/B test. What are some questions you can ask to get more information on what has already been measured, analyzed, or attempted? List them below...

1. Has viewing time been tracked in previous workshops?  
   If baseline data exists, we can determine whether the control group's average is typical or not before attributing any difference to the slide redesign.

2. Has a slide redesign been attempted before, and what were the results?
   Understanding prior iterations prevents us from re-testing something that was already ruled out, and helps us understand whether a 2-minute drop in viewing time is relative to past experiments.

3. Were both groups drawn from the same employee population, and were they randomly assigned?  
   If the control and treatment groups differ in role, department, or seniority, the viewing time difference may reflect the group rather than the slide redesign.



## Goal Alignment 
Align with stakeholders on what success means before interpreting any numbers.

1. What does a "successful" redesign look like to you? Shorter viewing times, longer viewing times, or something else entirely?  
   This is the most critical question. A shorter viewing time could mean employees absorbed the material efficiently, *or* that they disengaged early. We need to know which outcome the stakeholder values before we frame the results as positive or negative.

2. Is the goal of the redesign to improve learning outcomes, employee engagement, or both?  
   If the goal is comprehension, viewing time alone is a weak proxy. If the goal is keeping employees engaged longer, then the treatment's shorter viewing time may actually indicate a problem.

3. Is there a minimum viewing time threshold below which engagement is considered insufficient? 
   If stakeholders believe employees need at least 10 minutes to absorb the content, a mean of 9.9 minutes in the treatment group may be a concern,  even if the statistical test shows a significant difference.

## Define Over-performance 
Consider what it would mean if the treatment dramatically exceeded expectations.

1. If the redesigned slides produced significantly higher engagement (e.g., employees watched 50% longer), would the company be prepared to redesign all existing workshop materials? 
   Over performance creates its own operational questions. Knowing the stakeholder's capacity for change helps set realistic expectations for what the analysis can actually drive.

2. Is there a point at which viewing time becomes "too long" suggesting employees are confused or overwhelmed rather than engaged? 
   A dramatic increase in viewing time is not automatically good. If the treatment group spent much more time on slides, it could indicate the redesign made content harder to digest.

## Data Sourcing
Reflect on what other data sources you can use to answer your stakeholders exploratory goals.

1. Post workshop quiz or assessment scores  
   Viewing time measures exposure, but quiz scores measure retention. Combining both would allow us to ask: *did the treatment group learn as much in less time?* Which is a much stronger argument for the redesign.

2. Employee satisfaction or feedback surveys
   Self-reported engagement ("Did you find this workshop useful?") adds a qualitative layer to the quantitative viewing time data, helping distinguish efficient engagement from disengagement.
